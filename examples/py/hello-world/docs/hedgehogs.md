# Hedgehogs

Hedgehogs are small, nocturnal mammals best known for the thousands of stiff spines that cover their backs. There are seventeen living species spread across Europe, Asia, Africa, and parts of New Zealand, where they were introduced by British settlers in the nineteenth century.

Hedgehogs live in hedgerows, gardens, and woodland edges across Europe, foraging at night for beetles, slugs, earthworms, and the occasional windfall fruit. They prefer quiet, undisturbed cover during the day, curling into a tight ball of spines whenever they feel threatened.

In the wild a hedgehog will wander up to two kilometres a night in search of food, which makes continuous hedgerows and gaps in garden fences surprisingly important for their survival. In Britain the population has declined sharply since the turn of the century, and simple measures such as leaving wild patches and cutting hedgehog-sized holes between gardens have become a standard conservation tip.
